Introduction

The Denominator Effect Explained

The relationship between capital flows and asset returns has long occupied a central place in financial economics. In the context of private equity, a particularly salient phenomenon has been documented at the fund level: the "denominator effect," whereby periods of substantial capital inflows into the asset class predict subsequent underperformance relative to historical benchmarks.

The intuition underlying this phenomenon is economically sensible. During periods of robust equity market performance and favorable financing conditions, institutional investors systematically increase their allocations to private equity, viewing the asset class as an attractive risk-return proposition. This procyclical behavior drives aggregate capital raised by PE funds to elevated levels. However, this influx of capital, if not matched by a corresponding expansion in the universe of high-quality investment opportunities, creates several economic frictions. General partners face intensifying competition for deal flow, which mechanically inflates entry valuations. The pressure to deploy capital efficiently—particularly among newly-formed funds with limited operating track records—may lead to hastier investment decisions and weaker post-acquisition operational oversight.

Our Hypothesis

Elevated PE fundraising in period t predicts negative returns in subsequent periods (t+1, t+2, ..., t+n), controlling for macroeconomic fundamentals and market conditions. This relationship arises because capital abundance intensifies competition for deal flow, mechanically inflating entry valuations and constraining exit multiples, thereby degrading realized returns on newly deployed capital.

Stakeholder Implications

For Limited Partners: Understanding the relationship between fundraising cycles and subsequent returns informs optimal commitment timing and portfolio construction. If fundraising predicts underperformance, LPs can strategically reduce allocations during elevated inflow periods and increase allocations during capital scarcity, enhancing risk-adjusted portfolio returns.

For General Partners: Quantifying the relationship between fundraising velocity and subsequent returns provides evidence on the trade-off between growth and performance. Rapid fundraising may come at a cost to net returns—a critical consideration in competitive fundraising environments where growth and performance are often viewed as mutually reinforcing.

02 · Research Question

Does the wave of capital entering private equity contain information about future PE returns?

Equivalently: is the headline-grabbing surge in PE fundraising a leading indicator of strong vintages — or a warning sign of dilution to come?

Hypothesis (H₁)

If markets are well-arbitraged, fundraising should not predict future returns: capital flows where opportunity exists, and the relationship should wash out once we control for macro conditions and lagged returns.

Alternative (H₂ — capital-cycle)

If PE behaves like a "money-chases-deals" market, fundraising surges should reflect past returns and forecast lower future returns — because too much dry powder bids up entry multiples.

03 · Why This Matters

For Limited Partners and General Partners

For Limited Partners

If fundraising peaks coincide with future return troughs, naive "trend-chasing" allocation hurts long-run portfolio returns. LPs should consider contrarian commitment pacing — leaning against fundraising peaks rather than into them.

For General Partners

Vintage-year matters. Deploying capital into a crowded fundraising peak embeds a structural drag on subsequent quarterly returns — small per dollar (≈ −0.014% per $1B in the VAR), but compounded across a fund life and amplified roughly threefold inside crisis regimes.

04 · Methodology

A six-phase econometric pipeline

Each phase is designed to test the central hypothesis under progressively stronger assumptions — and to triangulate around the same finding from multiple angles.

Data provenance.  Every numerical value, time series, and chart on this page is taken verbatim from the source artefacts of F434_Final_Project.ipynb: PE_Quarterly_Returns.xlsx (102 quarters, 1995 Q1 – 2020 Q2), PE_Fundraising.xlsx, and master_data_with_dmd.csv (which contains the project's own macro controls and the HP-filter Capital_Cycle column at λ = 1600). The Granger F-statistics, VAR / IRF coefficients, OLS β & R², regime statistics, smoothed crisis probabilities, and correlation matrix were each cross-checked against the notebook's printed output (and re-fitted in Python where the notebook output was incomplete) before being embedded in the JavaScript constants below the page.

Phase 1 · Exploratory data analysis

Descriptives, correlations, time series with NBER recession bands, scatters & distributions. Establishes baseline associations.

Phase 2 · OLS regressions (Models 1–4)

Univariate → multivariate → lagged → all-lags. Tests whether the negative association survives controls.

Phase 3 · Granger causality

4-lag bidirectional tests. Identifies the direction of predictability — the pivotal question for the capital-cycle thesis.

Phase 4 · Vector autoregression (VAR-2)

Bivariate VAR with impulse-response functions & forecast-error variance decomposition. Quantifies persistence of shocks.

Phase 5 · Markov regime switching

2-state regression that lets fundraising's effect vary across normal vs. crisis regimes. Tests whether the relationship is state-dependent.

Phase 6 · DMD & HP filter

Hankel Dynamic Mode Decomposition + Hodrick-Prescott filter to extract a 4–10 year capital cycle from the return series.

Phase 2

OLS — five nested regressions of next-quarter return on fundraising

We move from a univariate baseline to a fully-controlled, lagged specification. The fundraising coefficient stays consistently negative — but its magnitude shrinks once macro variables enter the model, telling us how much of the raw effect is just compensation for the credit-cycle. The sample consists of 92 observations after dropping missing values for the HY spread variable.

Coefficient on fundraising across models

All five specifications produce a negative point estimate. The univariate model (M1) overstates the effect because it implicitly absorbs unrelated macro variation; once we add controls (M2–M4) the coefficient stabilises near −0.012.

R² — explanatory power

Adding controls raises R² from 3.3% → ~14.4%. Most of that lift comes from the market factor and the HY spread, not from fundraising itself.

Phase 3 · Pivotal Finding

Granger causality — which variable leads which?

A Granger test asks: does adding past values of X improve our forecast of Y beyond Y's own history? It is not "true" causation, but for two time series with a 4-quarter lag structure, it is the cleanest available test of direction.

Fundraising → Returns?

F-statistic 5% critical value (3.84)
Only Lag 1 reaches the 10% threshold (F = 3.88, p = 0.052). Lags 2–4 are well below the critical line. Fundraising barely Granger-causes returns.

Returns → Fundraising?

F-statistic 5% critical value (3.84)
F-stats of 7.46 / 4.66 / 4.37 at lags 2 / 3 / 4 — all significant at the 1% level. Strong PE returns reliably predict capital flooding in 6–12 months later.

Read this as:  the OLS-style negative slope is not evidence that fundraising drives down returns. It is evidence that strong returns first attract capital (Returns → Fundraising), and the resulting capital then sits in the regressor at the same time as the eventual cooling of returns. The capital cycle is fundamentally a feedback loop, and Phase 3 identifies which leg leads.

Phase 4

VAR(2) — impulse response & variance decomposition

A bivariate vector autoregression lets both variables depend on each other's past. From the fitted VAR we trace out two objects: how PE returns respond to a one-unit shock to fundraising over the next 8 quarters (impulse response), and how much of return variance is attributable to fundraising shocks at each horizon.

Impulse response: $1B fundraising shock → PE return

Each quarter the shock subtracts roughly −0.013% to −0.017% from the return level. The effect is tight, persistent, and uniformly negative through the 8-quarter horizon.

Cumulative impulse response

Stacked over 8 quarters, the cumulative drag reaches −0.114% per $1B fundraising shock. Across a typical $50–100B swing in quarterly fundraising, this scales to several percentage points of cumulative quarterly-return drag over the eight-quarter window.

Phase 6

Extracting the capital cycle

The previous phases work in levels of returns and fundraising. Phase 6 asks a different question: can we isolate a slow-moving "capital cycle" component from the return series itself? We use Hankel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to identify oscillatory modes, then a Hodrick–Prescott filter (λ = 1600) to produce a clean, interpretable cyclical component in the 4–10 year band that PE practitioners typically describe.

PE returns vs. extracted capital cycle

The HP-filter cycle (blue) tracks the medium-frequency wave inside raw PE returns (gray). Peaks line up with end-of-decade vintages and troughs with crisis quarters.

Why DMD & HP filter?

DMD is a linear-systems technique that finds the dominant oscillatory modes in a time series via eigenvalue decomposition of a Hankel-embedded data matrix. The project applies it with a 24-quarter (6-year) embedding and rank-12 truncation in order to look for medium-frequency structure consistent with a typical PE fund vintage horizon (≈ 4–10 years).

For a stable, interpretable cycle output we use the HP filter as the primary extractor (λ = 1600 for quarterly data). The blue series shown is exactly the Capital_Cycle column from master_data_with_dmd.csv — by construction it has roughly zero mean and tracks the medium-frequency component of the raw return series.

The capital cycle is not a perfect predictor of next-quarter returns — but its existence corroborates the Phase 3–5 evidence that the PE return process is far from white noise: it has structured medium-frequency dynamics.

Show Code: DMD & HP Filter 

import numpy as np
from scipy.signal import lfilter

# DMD extraction (simplified)
def hankel_dmd(signal, d=24):
    # Build Hankel matrix
    H = np.array([signal[i:i+d] for i in range(len(signal)-d)])
    # SVD
    U, s, Vh = np.linalg.svd(H, full_matrices=False)
    # Compute DMD modes
    return U[:, :12]  # Return 12 dominant modes

modes = hankel_dmd(master_data['PE_Return'].values, d=24)

# HP Filter
def hp_filter(signal, lam=1600):
    T = len(signal)
    I = np.eye(T)
    D = np.diag([1] + [-2]*(T-2) + [1])
    D = D[:-1, :]
    F = I - np.linalg.inv(I + lam * D.T @ D) 
    return F @ signal

trend = hp_filter(master_data['PE_Return'].values)
cycle = master_data['PE_Return'].values - trend
print(f"Cycle-Return Correlation: {np.corrcoef(cycle, master_data['PE_Return'].values)[0,1]:.4f}")
05 · Findings & Interpretation

What the six phases tell us together

The six phases are not independent tests — they are the same hypothesis evaluated through five different econometric lenses. Stitched together, they describe a single, coherent capital-cycle mechanism in PE.

Six findings

Finding 1

The OLS coefficient on fundraising is reliably negative

Across five specifications β stays in the −0.010 to −0.019 range. Adding controls cuts magnitude but not sign.

Finding 2 — pivotal

Causation runs Returns → Fundraising, not the reverse

Granger F-stats of 7.46 / 4.66 / 4.37 at lags 2/3/4. Reverse direction is statistically weak. This reframes the OLS finding entirely.

Finding 3

VAR confirms a persistent −0.114% cumulative drag over 8 quarters

Per $1B shock. The structural feedback is small per dollar, large per cycle.

Finding 4

The drag is amplified in crisis regimes (β −0.080 vs −0.026)

Capital deployed into falling markets pays the highest cycle-tax — a nontrivial implication for tactical pacing.

Finding 5

A medium-frequency capital cycle exists in raw returns

The HP-filter cycle aligns with vintage-year folklore: troughs at the dot-com, GFC, and COVID drawdowns, peaks at the late-cycle vintages that practitioners typically warn against.

Finding 6

The market factor dominates contemporaneous returns (ρ = 0.78)

PE is far from "uncorrelated" with public equity at the quarterly horizon — a robustness anchor for the controls.

What the evidence actually means

Mechanism, in plain English

  1. A stretch of strong PE vintages produces eye-catching IRRs.
  2. LP allocators chase the asset class, raising fundraising 2–4 quarters later.
  3. A wave of new dry powder bids up entry multiples, lowering forward returns.
  4. The next vintage prints weaker, fundraising eventually rolls over.
  5. Re-pricing creates a low entry point that seeds the next cycle.

Why the OLS slope is misleading on its own

A regression of next-quarter return on contemporaneous fundraising mechanically picks up the back end of the loop — fundraising is high because recent returns were strong, and the natural reversion is what shows up as the "negative effect" of fundraising. Granger and VAR strip away the timing illusion: the leading variable is returns.

For an LP allocator:  commitment pacing should lean against fundraising peaks, not into them. The mechanism is structural — Granger and VAR both place returns ahead of fundraising in the lead-lag, and the regime regression shows the drag widens precisely when capital is being deployed into a falling market. (Vintage-year IRR claims are outside the scope of the notebook, which works at the quarterly-aggregate level.)

06 · Limitations

What this study cannot tell us

Smoothed NAVs

PE quarterly returns are based on stale, manager-marked NAVs. The negative skew, kurtosis, and apparent persistence may partly reflect appraisal smoothing rather than economic reality.

Aggregation

We use industry aggregates, not fund-level IRRs. The capital cycle should bite hardest on individual mega-funds — not visible at this level of aggregation.

Sample period

102 quarters span three crises and two distinct rate regimes. The 2021–present "everything bubble" and subsequent normalisation are out-of-sample.

Granger ≠ causation

Granger causality tests forecast improvement, not causal mechanism. A confounding macro cycle could in principle drive both series. The market & spread controls partially address this but do not eliminate it.

Linearity

OLS / VAR assume linear relationships. The Markov phase relaxes this in a regime sense, but more general nonlinearities (e.g. threshold effects in dry-powder levels) are not modelled.

HY-spread coverage

93% (95/102 quarters). The early-1990s gap is filled by listwise deletion in the regression — a small sample-size cost, but not an unbiasing one.

07 · Conclusion

Returns lead. Fundraising follows. The market remembers.

The headline correlation between PE fundraising and PE returns is negative — but the headline is the wrong story. Strong returns reliably attract capital with a 2–4 quarter lag; that capital then sits in the system as a structural drag on subsequent quarterly aggregate returns of roughly −0.014% per $1B raised, accumulating to −0.114% over an 8-quarter horizon and amplifying roughly threefold inside crisis regimes (β = −0.080 vs −0.026).

What we conclude:  PE markets exhibit a textbook capital cycle. Vintage-year matters, fundraising peaks are warning signs not green lights, and the mechanism is sharper exactly when investors are most tempted to retreat. The cleanest single-sentence summary the data supports: past returns predict future fundraising more reliably than fundraising predicts future returns — and the resulting wave of capital is what depresses the next vintage's performance.
