01 · Introduction

Introduction

The Denominator Effect Explained

The relationship between capital flows and asset returns has long occupied a central place in financial economics. In the context of private equity, a particularly salient phenomenon has been documented at the fund level: the "denominator effect," whereby periods of substantial capital inflows into the asset class predict subsequent underperformance relative to historical benchmarks.

The intuition underlying this phenomenon is economically sensible. During periods of robust equity market performance and favorable financing conditions, institutional investors systematically increase their allocations to private equity, viewing the asset class as an attractive risk-return proposition. This procyclical behavior drives aggregate capital raised by PE funds to elevated levels. However, this influx of capital, if not matched by a corresponding expansion in the universe of high-quality investment opportunities, creates several economic frictions. General partners face intensifying competition for deal flow, which mechanically inflates entry valuations. The pressure to deploy capital efficiently—particularly among newly-formed funds with limited operating track records—may lead to hastier investment decisions and weaker post-acquisition operational oversight.

Our Hypothesis

H₁ (capital-cycle):  Elevated PE fundraising in period t predicts negative returns in subsequent periods (t+1, t+2, ..., t+n), controlling for macroeconomic fundamentals and market conditions. This relationship arises because capital abundance intensifies competition for deal flow, mechanically inflating entry valuations and constraining exit multiples, thereby degrading realized returns on newly deployed capital.

Alternative Hypothesis

H₀ (null / efficient-markets):  Elevated PE fundraising in period t carries no information about returns in subsequent periods (t+1, t+2, ..., t+n) once macroeconomic fundamentals and market conditions are controlled for. Under this view capital flows where opportunity exists, entry valuations are competitively priced, and any apparent fundraising–return relationship reflects common exposure to macro factors rather than a structural drag on newly deployed capital.

Stakeholder Implications

For Limited Partners: Understanding the relationship between fundraising cycles and subsequent returns informs optimal commitment timing and portfolio construction. If fundraising predicts underperformance, LPs can strategically reduce allocations during elevated inflow periods and increase allocations during capital scarcity, enhancing risk-adjusted portfolio returns.

For General Partners: Quantifying the relationship between fundraising velocity and subsequent returns provides evidence on the trade-off between growth and performance. Rapid fundraising may come at a cost to net returns—a critical consideration in competitive fundraising environments where growth and performance are often viewed as mutually reinforcing.

02 · Methodology

A Roadmap of Our Methodology

How the four phases fit together.  Our analysis runs in four phases, with each one taking a different modeling approach. We start with exploratory data analysis to map the basic correlations, then move into OLS regressions to see whether the negative fundraising-return relationship remains significant after removing confounding variables. Granger causality tests pin down which variable leads the other, and a VAR(2) traces how a fundraising shock propagates through future returns. Two supplementary analyses — a DMD and HP-filter capital-cycle extraction, and a Markov regime-switching regression that tests whether the effect is stable across normal and crisis periods — are included in the Appendix.

Phase 1 · Exploratory Data Analysis

Descriptives, correlations, time series with NBER recession bands, scatters & distributions. Establishes baseline associations.

Phase 2 · OLS Regressions (Models 1–4)

Univariate → multivariate → lagged → all-lags. Tests whether the negative association survives controls.

Phase 3 · Granger Causality

4-lag bidirectional tests. Identifies the direction of predictability — the pivotal question for the capital-cycle thesis.

Phase 4 · Vector Autoregression (VAR-2)

Bivariate VAR with impulse-response functions & forecast-error variance decomposition. Quantifies persistence of shocks.

This comovement is consistent with reverse causality—good returns prompt LP commitments, while market downturns reduce both returns and future fundraising simultaneously.

04 · Ordinary Least Squares Regression

Ordinary Least Squares Regression — Five Nested Regressions of Next-Quarter Return on Fundraising

We move from a univariate baseline to a fully-controlled model that also incorporates prior-quarter fundraising terms. The fundraising coefficient stays consistently negative — but its magnitude shrinks once macro variables enter the model, telling us how much of the raw effect is just compensation for the credit-cycle. The sample consists of 92 observations after dropping missing values for the HY spread variable.

Coefficient on Fundraising Across Models

All five specifications produce a negative point estimate. The univariate model (M1) overstates the effect because it implicitly absorbs unrelated macro variation; once we add controls (M2–M4) the coefficient stabilises near −0.012.

R² — Explanatory Power

Adding controls raises R² from 3.3% → ~14.4%. Most of that lift comes from the market factor and the HY spread, not from fundraising itself.

Regression Results

Model Specification β (Fundraising) R² Reads as…

What we still don't know:  OLS tells us fundraising and next-quarter returns co-move slightly negatively. It cannot tell us whether fundraising causes the lower return, or whether something else (e.g. last quarter's strong return) drives both.

05 · Granger Causality

Granger Causality — Which Variable Leads Which?

A Granger test asks: does adding past values of X improve our forecast of Y beyond Y's own history? It is not "true" causation, but for two time series with a 4-quarter lag structure, it is the cleanest available test of direction.

Fundraising → Returns?

F-statistic 5% critical value (3.84)
Only Lag 1 reaches the 10% threshold (F = 3.88, p = 0.052). Lags 2–4 are well below the critical line. Fundraising barely Granger-causes returns.

Returns → Fundraising?

F-statistic 5% critical value (3.84)
F-stats of 7.46 / 4.66 / 4.37 at lags 2 / 3 / 4 — all significant at the 1% level. Strong PE returns reliably predict capital flooding in 6–12 months later.

Key Takeaway:  the OLS-style negative slope is not evidence that fundraising drives down returns. It is evidence that strong returns first attract capital (Returns → Fundraising), and the resulting capital then sits in the regressor at the same time as the eventual cooling of returns. The capital cycle is fundamentally a feedback loop, and Phase 3 identifies which leg leads.

06 · VAR & Impulse Response

VAR(2) — Impulse Response & Variance Decomposition

A bivariate vector autoregression lets both variables depend on each other's past. From the fitted VAR we trace out two objects: how PE returns respond to a one-unit shock to fundraising over the next 8 quarters (impulse response), and how much of return variance is attributable to fundraising shocks at each horizon.

Impulse Response: $1B Fundraising Shock → PE Return

Each quarter the shock subtracts roughly −0.012% to −0.017% from the return level. The effect is tight, persistent, and uniformly negative through the 8-quarter horizon.

Cumulative Impulse Response

Stacked over 8 quarters, the cumulative drag reaches −0.114% per $1B fundraising shock. Across a typical $50–100B swing in quarterly fundraising, this scales to several percentage points of cumulative quarterly-return drag over the eight-quarter window.

from statsmodels.tsa.api import VAR

# Prepare VAR data
var_data = master_data[['PE_Return', 'PE_Fundraising']].copy()
for col in var_data.columns:
    var_data[col] = pd.to_numeric(var_data[col], errors='coerce')
var_data = var_data.dropna()

# Fit VAR(2)
model      = VAR(var_data)
var_result = model.fit(2)
print(var_result.summary())

# Impulse response: $1B fundraising shock → PE return path
irf = var_result.irf(8)
fundraising_shock_to_returns = irf.irfs[:, 0, 1]   # response of var 0 to shock in var 1

07 · Findings & Interpretation

What the Four Phases Tell Us Together

The four phases are not independent tests — they are the same hypothesis evaluated through four different econometric lenses. Stitched together, they describe a single, coherent capital-cycle mechanism in PE.

Four Findings

Finding 1Phase 1 · Exploratory Data Analysis

The Market Factor Dominates Contemporaneous Returns (ρ = 0.78)

PE is far from "uncorrelated" with public equity at the quarterly horizon — a robustness anchor for the controls.

Finding 2Phase 2 · OLS Regressions

The OLS Coefficient on Fundraising Is Reliably Negative

Across five specifications β stays in the −0.010 to −0.019 range. Adding controls cuts magnitude but not sign.

Finding 3 — pivotalPhase 3 · Granger Causality

Causation Runs Returns → Fundraising, Not the Reverse

Granger F-stats of 7.46 / 4.66 / 4.37 at lags 2/3/4. Reverse direction is statistically weak. This reframes the OLS finding entirely.

Finding 4Phase 4 · Vector Autoregression (VAR-2)

VAR Confirms a Persistent −0.114% Cumulative Drag Over 8 Quarters

Per $1B shock. The structural feedback is small per dollar, large per cycle.

What the Evidence Actually Means

Mechanism, in Plain English

  1. A stretch of strong PE vintages produces eye-catching IRRs.
  2. LP allocators chase the asset class, raising fundraising 2–4 quarters later.
  3. A wave of new dry powder bids up entry multiples, lowering forward returns.
  4. The next vintage prints weaker, fundraising eventually rolls over.
  5. Re-pricing creates a low entry point that seeds the next cycle.

Why the OLS Slope Is Misleading on Its Own

A regression of next-quarter return on contemporaneous fundraising mechanically picks up the back end of the loop — fundraising is high because recent returns were strong, and the natural reversion is what shows up as the "negative effect" of fundraising. Granger and VAR strip away the timing illusion: the leading variable is returns.

For an LP allocator:  commitment pacing should lean against fundraising peaks, not into them. The mechanism is structural — Granger and VAR both place returns ahead of fundraising in the lead-lag, and the cumulative drag of −0.114% per $1B over eight quarters scales meaningfully against typical fundraising swings. (Vintage-year IRR claims are outside the scope of the notebook, which works at the quarterly-aggregate level.)

08 · Limitations

Limitations

Smoothed NAVs

PE quarterly returns are based on stale, manager-marked NAVs. The negative skew, kurtosis, and apparent persistence may partly reflect appraisal smoothing rather than economic reality.

Aggregation

We use industry aggregates, not fund-level IRRs. The capital cycle should bite hardest on individual mega-funds — not visible at this level of aggregation.

Sample Period

102 quarters span three crises and two distinct rate regimes. The 2021–present "everything bubble" and subsequent normalisation are out-of-sample.

Granger ≠ Causation

Granger causality tests forecast improvement, not causal mechanism. A confounding macro cycle could in principle drive both series. The market & spread controls partially address this but do not eliminate it.

Linearity

OLS / VAR assume linear relationships. The Markov regime-switching analysis in the Appendix relaxes this in a regime sense, but more general nonlinearities (e.g. threshold effects in dry-powder levels) are not modelled.

HY-Spread Coverage

93% (95/102 quarters). The early-1990s gap is filled by listwise deletion in the regression — a small sample-size cost, but not an unbiasing one.

09 · Conclusion

Conclusion

Our OLS regressions show that fundraising loads negatively on next-quarter PE returns, with β between −0.010 and −0.019 and a cumulative drag of about −0.114% per $1B over eight quarters, which is consistent with the denominator-effect intuition we set out to test. The magnitude is modest, though, and once the Granger tests come in it's clear the more interesting result is the other direction: past returns predict future fundraising at the 1% level across multiple lags, while fundraising's ability to predict returns is weak. Most of what shows up as "fundraising depressing returns" in the OLS is really just lagged capital chasing — LPs commit 6–12 months after a strong vintage, and that capital lands right when the run-up is mechanically due to cool off.

B · Capital Cycles

Extracting the Capital Cycle

The main report works in levels of returns and fundraising. This supplementary analysis asks a different question: can we isolate a slow-moving "capital cycle" component from the return series itself? We use Hankel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to identify oscillatory modes, then a Hodrick–Prescott filter (λ = 1600) to produce a clean, interpretable cyclical component in the 4–10 year band that PE practitioners typically describe.

PE Returns vs. Extracted Capital Cycle

The HP-filter cycle (blue) tracks the medium-frequency wave inside raw PE returns (gray). Peaks line up with end-of-decade vintages and troughs with crisis quarters.

Why DMD & HP Filter?

DMD is a linear-systems technique that finds the dominant oscillatory modes in a time series via eigenvalue decomposition of a Hankel-embedded data matrix. The project applies it with a 24-quarter (6-year) embedding and rank-12 truncation in order to look for medium-frequency structure consistent with a typical PE fund vintage horizon (≈ 4–10 years).

For a stable, interpretable cycle output we use the HP filter as the primary extractor (λ = 1600 for quarterly data). The blue series shown is exactly the Capital_Cycle column from master_data_with_dmd.csv — by construction it has roughly zero mean and tracks the medium-frequency component of the raw return series.

The capital cycle is not a perfect predictor of next-quarter returns — but its existence corroborates the main-report evidence that the PE return process is far from white noise: it has structured medium-frequency dynamics.

Show Code: Hankel DMD + HP Filter Capital-Cycle Extraction 

from numpy.linalg import svd, eig, pinv

pe_returns = master_data['PE_Return'].astype(float).values

# ---- Hankel DMD ---------------------------------------------------
def hankelize(x, d):
    """Time-delay embedding."""
    x = np.asarray(x).ravel()
    T = len(x)
    return np.stack([x[i : i + (T - d + 1)] for i in range(d)], axis=0)

def exact_dmd_simple(X, Xp, r=None, dt=1.0):
    U, s, Vt = svd(X, full_matrices=False)
    if r is None:
        r = int((s / s[0] > 1e-10).sum())
    r = min(r, len(s))
    Ur, sr, Vr = U[:, :r], s[:r], Vt[:r, :]
    A_tilde = Ur.conj().T @ Xp @ Vr.T @ np.diag(1.0 / sr)
    lam, W  = eig(A_tilde)
    Phi     = Xp @ Vr.T @ np.diag(1.0 / sr) @ W
    b       = pinv(Phi) @ X[:, 0]
    omega   = np.log(lam.astype(complex)) / dt
    return {'Phi': Phi, 'lam': lam, 'omega': omega, 'b': b, 'rank': r}

x_centered = pe_returns - pe_returns.mean()
H  = hankelize(x_centered, d=24)        # 24-quarter (6-yr) embedding
X  = H[:, :-1]
Xp = H[:, 1:]
dmd = exact_dmd_simple(X, Xp, r=12, dt=0.25)

# Identify modes whose period falls in the 4–10 year capital-cycle band
periods    = np.abs(2 * np.pi /
                    np.where(np.abs(dmd['omega'].imag) > 1e-8,
                             dmd['omega'].imag, np.inf))
amplitudes = np.abs(dmd['b'])
band_modes = np.where((periods >= 4) & (periods <= 10))[0]
for idx in band_modes:
    print(f"  Mode {idx}: period={periods[idx]:.2f} yrs, "
          f"amplitude={amplitudes[idx]:.4f}")

# ---- HP filter (manual implementation, λ = 1600) ------------------
lambd = 1600
n     = len(pe_returns)
D2    = np.diag(np.ones(n-1), 1) - 2*np.diag(np.ones(n)) + np.diag(np.ones(n-1), -1)
D2    = D2[:-2, :]
I     = np.eye(n)
trend          = np.linalg.solve(I + lambd * D2.T @ D2, pe_returns)
capital_cycle  = pe_returns - trend
master_data['Capital_Cycle'] = capital_cycle

print(f"HP Trend:        mean={trend.mean():.4f}%,  std={trend.std():.4f}%")
print(f"Capital Cycle:  mean={capital_cycle.mean():.4f}%,  std={capital_cycle.std():.4f}%")
print(f"Cycle ↔ Returns correlation: "
      f"{np.corrcoef(capital_cycle, pe_returns)[0, 1]:.4f}")

# Plot returns vs extracted capital cycle
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 5))
q = np.arange(len(pe_returns))
ax.plot(q, pe_returns, color='#1f77b4', linewidth=1.5, alpha=0.7,
        label='PE Returns')
ax.plot(q, capital_cycle, color='#ff7f0e', linewidth=2.5,
        label='Capital Cycle (HP, 4–10 yr band)')
ax.fill_between(q, capital_cycle, 0, alpha=0.15, color='#ff7f0e')
